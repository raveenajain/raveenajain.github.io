Fanworks
Introduction
For my Information Architecture class, we got to pick a topic to design our coursework around. If you know me, then it's not a surprise that I ended up with something fandom related; in this case, I chose fanworks as my domain. While there were some restraints on the project (i.e. the number of wireframes and the word count), it really was up to us to, as the brief explained, "design the [Information Architecture] of a web-based information environment." While there are a number of websites, ranging from social to archival, that allow users to find and post fanworks, my goal was to create something that would bridge the disconnect that forms within fandoms because of how spread across the internet they are. Aside from having a fun topic, I also enjoyed finding ways of organizing complicated information into something usable. For each of the following sections, I have included the corresponding deliverable (all were made on Figma) and condensed versions of the design justifications that made up my coursework submission.
Domain Model and Expert Interviews
I began by interviewing three Domain Experts and performing an informal review of the well-known fanfiction website, Archive of Our Own (AO3). AO3 was useful as a database and as a way of understanding how people customize their fandom experience through user-generated tagging. From my interviews, it became clear that the fandoms themself dictate what fanworks hold value. While the overall goal of the Doman Model was to identify core concepts and their relationships between each other, I saw building off of the Hub and Spoke structure that focused on a “central concept” (Brown, 2011, pp. 80) as the best approach to accommodate the diversity that exists across fanworks and fandoms. Overlap between AO3 and expert interviews made it possible to determine which elements were expected across fandoms and fanworks. Another core takeaway from my research was the emphasis experts put on the ability to block/exclude certain filters/tags. Because fans shape their fandom experiences, folksonomies are often prioritized over structured data (Rosenfeld et al, 2015, pp. 128). And considering how fandom elements are interconnected, using Brown’s advice on “reducing triangles” became vital in helping me determine core relationships (2011, pp. 82).
Sitemap
I used Card Sorting (on Optimal Workshop) to see if potential users would be able to condense the 12 types of fandoms from AO3 (alongside others I had added). Due to the variation in fandom experiences, my hypothesis that this Card Sort might not help me refine these groupings was correct. My “ah-ha moment” was realizing that the only entities in my Domain Model feeding directly into fanworks were fandoms and creators. This led me to using the hybrid Hierarchical and Database pattern, as the user would constantly come across “larger volumes of information,” and to drawing upon Spencer’s “basic shapes for drawing an IA,” especially her “part of a page” notation (Spencer, 2010, pp. 180-193, 247). To confirm I was on the right track, I performed Tree Tests (on Optimal Workshop) that incorporated filtering. Because 50% of tasks were completed using the “Fandoms” navigation, which would link to thousands of results, I decided to breakdown that category so users looking to browse could have a more manageable experience. “Blended Results” exists for a similar reason; each category of result utilizes different facets and showing multiple on the same page could complicate the website for users. Creating this Sitemap allowed me to take the higher level concepts from the Domain Model and think about how they could be perceived from a user's perspective, making it much easier to create Wireframes later on.
User Journey
To tie into this website's primary goal, my user’s goal had to relate to finding fanworks, and building a scenario helped clarify the filters the user might incorporate. The journey itself was meant to showcase four potential ways a user could find fanart. Because every user’s background with fandom is different, I did not want to include only a preferred path, as that would not showcase the range of options. However, I highlighted what could be considered a preferred path based on number of steps and the specific attributes of the user as established by the goal. The User’s Thoughts were added to establish context and help emphasize why this journey might be preferred considering the user’s specific background with the Doctor Who fandom (Brown, 2011 pp. 140). I used Caddick and Cable’s design of diamond shaped decision points and pointed rectangles for steps (i.e. actions) (2011, pp. 83). As they used a rounded rectangle for the starting point (Caddick and Cable, 2011, pp. 83), which in my case was the Home page, I decided to use rounded rectangles to notate any page the user might reach. Making sure not to “cross the streams” (Brown, 2011 pp. 130) helped me organize the elements and structure of the journey itself. When a user is making a more significant step forward in the process, they move from left to right, but actions that don’t offer as much change to what the user sees are typically stacked.
Wireframes and Testing
I performed remote, Moderated Tests with three target users and created and posted a Click Test to Tumblr, targeting online users with a strong interest in fandoms and fanworks; 28 users completed this study. I took inspiration from Toub’s approach (2000) to help formulate my questions; to evaluate structure, grouping, and labeling, I used the goal from my User Journey as a prompt, asked about initial actions a user might take, and asked what users thought clicking on certain elements would do/show (Toub, 2000, pp. 14-23). To prevent users from being biased towards clicking on “Fandoms,” this dropdown was not initially shown; for similar reasons, filters applied on Screen 3 were unchecked. The Click Test and Moderated Testing results generally aligned; a low success rate from the Click Tests coupled with similar results from the Moderated Tests made a change higher priority. These wireframes have been slightly modified based on the results from User Testing.

Screen 1 – Home

As 2 of 3 Moderated Testing participants wanted to see new work from creators they had favourited, and as this builds a more personalized experience, a “Fandom of the Day” header was removed and “Some...Content” was added. All users during the Moderated Tests were able to vocalize what clicking on “By Fandom Type” would do, confirming this labeling choice. In the future, as users leaned more towards navigational breakdowns, I would like to explore making “Fanworks” a drop-down, using Card Sorting to see if overarching categories related to medium could be established. Additionally, I would do further testing to determine the best options for the “All Categories” drop-down as results from the Moderated Tests were inconclusive.

Screen 2 – Blended Results

While users can choose a category “to clarify their intent,” if they do not, because the results would not be “homogeneous,” they will see this Blended Results page (Russel-Rose and Tate, 2013, pp. 144). This approach was appropriate as there is no preferred path I want users to take and my goal is “to encourage [users] to engage in further exploration and discovery” (Russel-Rose and Tate, 2013, pp. 144). Moderated Testing participants indicated that they liked seeing this page as it made the results less overwhelming.

Screen 3 – All Results

The default state of each filter would be collapsed due to their size (Russel-Rose and Tate, 2013, pp. 173-174). “Fanworks, Platforms, and Languages” use traditional, squared checkboxes as users might want their results to, for example, contain fanfiction AND fanart. “Fandoms, Warnings, Characters, Tropes and Genres, and Ships” use a hybrid checkbox and radio button. The circular shape comes from the radio button to imply that an element can be included OR excluded; the check and X come from the checkbox and are used to show that multiple elements within a filter can be selected. Moderated Test participants successfully recognized how these filters would work. Because the list in “Characters” can become long, to limit scrolling, pagination was added and the elements were ordered via popularity (Russel-Rose and Tate, 2013, pp. 150). Since the use of folksonomies and study of tagging on AO3 means the number of “Specific References” could be incredibly large, and considering Russel-Rose and Tate’s explanation, AO3’s “Additional Tags” option, and the importance of inclusion and exclusion, I made use of Search Within (2013, pp. 103-104).

Screen 4 – Fanwork Page

Intrinsic, administrative, and descriptive metadata (Spencer, 2010, pp. 187) are all used and displayed to offer the user thorough information about any fanwork. However, inspired by the feedback from one of the Moderated Tests (the user felt as though there was not enough focus on the image), I would do a Five Second Test to see what information users are drawn to on this page (Booth, 2022) and then, once they have had more time to look at the page, ask them if that information aligns with what they value most. When asked where to find more information about the creator of this fanwork, 82% of Click Test participants clicked on “I_Met_DT” (expected) and 11% went to the Tumblr link. In the Moderated Tests, users went to “I_Met_DT,” Tumblr, or to Tumblr and then back to “I_Met_DT,” if unsatisfied. In the future, I would like to show users a Creator page before this one to see if that information would prompt more of them to remain on this website.
