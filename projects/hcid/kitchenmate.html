KitchenMate
First Steps
For my Interaction Design class, I worked in a small group to apply User Centered Design principles to develop an application (called KitchenMate) for the 10-inch Echo Show. Our goal, provided via the brief, was to encourage users to improve their "diets and [be] more mindful of the environmental impact of their food." Our first steps were to do some background research on the topic, meet to discuss that research, and to plan out a timeline. As we were working remotely, most of the screenshots included are from our Miro Board.
Interviews
Every design decision we would be making had to be based off of our interviews with potential users. As we would each be conducting semi-structured interviews, we decided on the overall topics we felt would be important to cover, wrote a few questions for each, and refined them together. After all the interviews and observations were finished, we wrote down key points for each user we had spoken to.
Deliverables (made via Miro)
By looking for common trends and overlapping ideas in our data, we were able to translate and scale down our information to a more cohesive List of Key Insights. This was incredibly useful in helping us create Personas, for both primary and secondary target users, and Future User Journeys for each of those Personas.

Creating Point of View Statements and, from those, How Might We Questions really helped us move forward from brainstorming to refining our ideas into actual features. We worked together to figure out both what would be core to include based on what our users had said as well as what was realistic and important to implement based on the brief we had received. To help with figuring out that implementation, we tied Functional, Non-Functional, and Data Requirements back to our POV Statements. And, throughout the entire process, we made sure to empathize with users wants and needs and to refer back to our initial user research.
Sketches
With a list of features we were looking to implement, we divided said features into overarching, key categories; each team member then took the lead on a couple of areas each (I focused on the features related to presenting recipes to the user and developing a rewards system to motivate them). We used low-fidelity sketching as a way of brainstorming and efficiently and effectively communicating ideas. (Only my sketches are included below.)
Wireframes and Clickable Prototype
The next step of the coursework required us to create mid-fidelity, annotated wireframes and to make those into a clickable prototype. We divided the work and prioritized designing the key features first before developing any additional screens. I also created a grid system and template page (for assets like buttons, text, etc.).
As none of the team had experience with Axure, we decided to use this software over Figma and took this as an opportunity to learn something new "on the job." When designing, considering different scenarios beyond our User Journeys was crucial in helping us understand how our application could be used so we could create something that would be accessible via both voice and touch while still aligning with the more minimalist interfaces typically seen on Smart Home devices.
Evaluation
To work with the resources our group had available, we used both in-person and remote moderated testing with target users (with a classic think-aloud protocol for both). We developed a set of tasks framed by a Scenario that was inspired by our Future User Journeys. For each participant, we wrote out the results on Miro sticky notes, differentiating between positive and negative feedback. We drew connections between the responses and considered frequency, effect on the product, as well as success rate when prioritizing our results.
